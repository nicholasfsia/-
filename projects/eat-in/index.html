Eat In

A mobile app that caters weekly recipes and shopping lists by learning from the user’s spending habits. PROJECT TYPE

Passion Project


ROLE

Product Designer


TOOLS

Hooked model, Figma, InVision


TIMELINE

2 weeks, currently is being revisited


TL;DR

Cooking at home has become a burden in this fast-paced life. Barriers such as unfamiliarity with cooking and time pressure lead young people to eat out often. This behaviour tend to be expensive and unhealthy. Solutions were explored using hooked model by Nir Eyal resulting in a meal planning app. The meal planning app caters weekly recipe and shopping list by learning from the user’s spending habit. The app main features includes varied weekly recipe and linked shopping list. This project is currently being revisited to focus more on breaking barrier of the unfamiliarity of cooking through food education.

Problems

Due to unfamiliarity of cooking and time pressure, young people tend to eat out or take out food almost all the time. While not only it cost a lot of money, this can lead to an unhealthy eating habit. In addition, for the people who are not familiar with cooking, the barriers to start cooking are bigger. From not having the right equipment to not knowing how to do things. Currently, most recipe apps or sites are to be a recipes database instead of helping the users to start cooking. With the “I have no time to cook” reason, cooking becomes a huge burden.

Goals

Business Goal

To help the user learn how to cook and plan their meals. Ideally, optimize user retention and gaining revenue by partnering with food brands and grocery stores.

User Goal

To create a healthier, cheaper, and non-time-consuming eating habit.

Requirements

From the goals, assumptions, and constraints, a set of requirements was define to measure the success of the project.

Onboarding

  • Must be able to connect to a credit card or more
  • Can connect to Mint app
  • If a user doesn’t want to connect to a credit card or Mint account there should be a fallback path
  • Ask for general questions regarding dietary restrictions, allergies, and how many people the user will cook for

User’s food spending learning

  • Track the total spending on food or restaurants
  • Able to categorize the uncategorized spendinge

Recipe and shopping list

  • Show a set of recipes on a weekly basis
  • The user should be able to remove and add a recipe from the set
  • Link the ingredients from each recipe to the shopping list
  • The user should be able to add their own shopping list manually

Easy to share

  • The user should be able to share the recipe with their social circle
  • The user should be able to share what they’ve made

Show progress

  • The app should show how much money can the user save by eating in
  • The app should show how much money the user has saved by eating in

Approach

Defining the users

Two type of young people were explored, young professionals and university students. Young professionals were chosen as the main focus. The assumption was young professionals are more aware of their spending and eating habit. Thus, the adoption rate is expected to be higher than students. For time reason, I want to focus more on the user that has a higher potential for adoption rate.


User research was done through observing my past co-op/internship co-workers. They almost always ate out for lunch. They frequently consumed food items that are simple to make at home, like salad, burrito bowl, sandwich, and roasted chicken. While it’s true eating out remove the necessity to spend time on cooking, however, during lunchtime, food places gets really busy causing them have to wait a long time anyway. They do however sometimes brought food from home, but they’re being inefficient about it (e.g. brought too many containers for a simple sandwich).

The Hook model

Since the goal is to change eating habit, I used Nir Eyal’s framework of the hook that is helpful to create a habit-forming-product. The hook model helped me to figure out what important features to add to the app. The features implemented in the prototype were all based on the hooked model.

Final Design

Hook it up

The onboarding of the app includes the credit card or Mint account integration and questions regarding the user. The idea of the card or Mint account integration is the app should be able to track what the user spends daily and record all the food-related purchases. This information will be used to know what kind of restaurant does the user like to go and how often does the user go there. Then this can be used to predict what cuisine do those restaurants provide and what’s the popular food there. This solution is heavily data-driven with the hope to obtain information about the user by tracking their food spending without the need of user constantly inputting information. By knowing this information, it can understand what kind of food the user may enjoy, thus can provide the recipes that the user likes. In addition, this step is not mandatory. If the user chooses to skip this step, the user will still be able to obtain weekly cooking plan but not as personalized.

Before a cooking plan was shown, basic questions such as dietary restrictions, allergies, and the number of people the user will cook for were asked. These questions and answers from the user will help the app to recommend a more personalized cooking plan.

Credit card or Mint integration

Cooking plan

The cooking plan itself consists of recipes for the whole week. The interaction model used is recipe card. Each recipe is displayed as a card and can be removed and added by the user. The user can remove the card by selecting the remove button at the bottom of each card. The user can add recipes by selecting the button at the bottom of this page. This add recipe will be generated by the app.

The cooking plan screens

Shopping list

The shopping list gathers ingredients from the recipes recommended for a weekly cooking plan. In addition, I decided to allow the user to add a new list for their own use. Thus, in this case, their shopping list can be united in Eat In app.

The shopping list screens

Recipe

When a card is selected, the user is led to that recipe page. Within it, there are details about the recipe (ingredients and how to), reviews, and photos of the food generated by the app users.


To see the review the user needs to tap on the top photo. Below it is the list of photos generated by Eat In users. The featured photo, which is the top photo can change depending on the popularity of the photo.

The recipe screens

Optimized for sharing

There are two ways to share a recipe. Through direct share or share to feed. The purpose of each sharing system is different. Direct share allows the user to share only the recipe to the user’s social circle through social media or messaging. The share button is placed right beside the recipe name. It’s placed there so that it’s obvious enough for the user to navigate to the button. All the information at the top of the recipe should be enough for the user to decide to share the recipe or not.

The direct share

In contrast, share to feed, This is pretty much adding the social factor of cooking at home to the app. Which is expected will encourage the user to “show of” what they can make at home. By sharing what they have made, their followers will see what they made and this is an indirect form of sharing the recipe. Through the feed, the user’s followers should be able to select the post and read the full recipe.


The upload button is placed at the end of that recipe page right underneath the last how to step. The idea is as the user reaches the last step, the cooking process is pretty much done and the user can easily select that button to share what they’ve just made.

Learning and Next Step

The target user might be very narrow. One suggestion is to implement more extensive questionnaires in the onboarding for a wider range of users. Progress like money saved, meals cooked, calories count etc. were not considered in the design and should be explored. Finally, how would the user know if the app uses the information from the Mint app or credit card? This project is currently brought back for a second iteration. This time I am collaborating with an engineer to support the ideation and technical side. Barriers, such as equipment absence, time pressures, and lack of cooking knowledge of the users are the main focus for the this iteration.
